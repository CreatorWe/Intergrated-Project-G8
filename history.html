Ancient Greece and Rome

What was Ancient Greece and Rome?

Ancient Greece and Rome were empires that ruled a massive amount of land and people. They infuenced and changed the world so much ranging from language, politics, infrastructure, education, and so much more.

Ancient Greece

The Begenning (Archaic Period | 700 - 480 B.C)

The Dark Age was gone. The Archaic Period is here. Greece was not yet an actually country, but was multiple independent city-states that would colonize other land in the mediterranean because the land where they reside is not suitable for agriculture. These city-states were very different, but they all had 1 thing in common; their economy was based on agriculture (thanks to their colonies). A city-state was also not ruled by a king, but instead was ruled by wealthy aristocrats. These period also was when greece started to have major advancements in art and poetry.

The Graeco-Persian Wars (499 - 449 B.C)

The Persian Empire was knocking at Greece's door. The war lasted 20 years. The Persians kept pressing on, but the military innovation of greece squashed these attempts of invasion. One particular battle was the Battle of Marathon. This was when the Athens used the battle formation "Phalanx"; it was where they would have heavily armored soldiers at the front and back covering every corner (including above) and spear troops inside. The Persians lost around 6400~ men and the Greeks lost only 193 men. Another was the Battle of Thermopylae. This was the famous battle wherein the 300 Spartans held back thousands of Persians. Another reason was the naval capability of Greece. The Greeks fought off the Persians at the sea at the Battle of Salamis. The Poleis (Polis; Ancient Greek City-States) banded together and fought off the Persians, single handedly defeated them from ever taking Greece.

The Short Union of Greece and The Peloponnesian War (477 - 404 B.C)

After defeating the Persians, Athens proposed to the other Poleis a sort of union wherein they would all pay for the same army which would provide protection to those who are in this union. Around 157 cities join this union. Athens was the central city/headquarters. However, Sparta and a few other cities didn't join, and grew jelous of the prosperity and wealth that the union brang to Athens and the other Poleis. In 431 B.C, the Spartans and their allies besieged and sacked Athens. After this, the allies of Athens began to break ties one by one after Alcibiades (the Demagogue of Athens after the sacking) fled to Sparta for political asylume after being humilated and shamed by the people of Athens (accused) for breaking off important parts of the Hermes Statue. Athens then signed a peace treaty at 404 B.C on the condition they destroy their city walls.

Alexander the Great, Hellnism, and the End of Ancient Greece. (405 - 30 B.C)

Alexander the Great went and conquered the entirity of the Persian Empire, but didn't stop there, and went on to conquer India. Cities were named after him, the culture flourshed and mixed making an entirely different way of living, and the captial was moving to Bablyon. When Alexander died, the Hellnism Period began. The leaders of this new great empire kept on fighting and losing agaisnt each other and those in the colonies. They continued to lose control over the land and suddenly the Romans appeared. In 146 B.C, the Romans took Greece, and in 30 B.C, they manage to take the last breath of Hellnism. However, even with the rule of Rome, the culture of Greece still managed to be alive and some of it was even adopted into the Roman life style. Because of this, it extended the life of Ancient Greece and it's culture.

Ancient Rome

The Beginning (753 - 509 B.C)

Rome! Rome was a small settlement by the river tiber. It's neighbors was the Etruscans to the north and Greek Colonies to the south. Instead of fighting each other, they traded instead and flourished. Rome was an opened city that accepted rejects like murderers, theives, runaway slaves, etc. These people and more get the oppertunity of being a fully roman citizen. Romans invented the aqueducts, Latin (inspired from the Greek Language), the roman calandar, concrete (not invent but made it more widspread), The romans were led by Kings, but after the sex scandal with the son of the king Lucius Tarquinius Superbus, he got expelled from Rome.

The Republic (509 - 287 B.C)

After the sex scandal, the romans decided not to have anymore kings/no more monarchs. They instead elected 2 consuls a year, which who were controlled by the senate; 300 'fathers' or Patricians. The patricians were the noble class in rome. they were the ones who held all of the positions in the governemnt and were the ones that would usually own land. Then there was the plebians; the commoners of rome. Those who were merchants, workers, and soldiers of rome. They didn't have that much rights and were not allowed to join the government except the military. This social conflict continued until 287 B.C. when something kown as Conflict of the Orders happened. The plebians waged a campaign wherein they made it as if they were seperate from the state, elected their own officials, and made their own records. This conflict was resolved when Quintus Hortensius, a plebian, was appointed. He made a law known as Lex Hortensia; plebiscites would be valid for the entire Roman population and would have the force of law. This put an end to the social conflict and gave equal rights to all in rome, giving birth the Republic; a government for the people and with the people.

The Expansion and Greece. (390 - 146 B.C)

The romans after reorganizing and reforming ther military, starting taking more and more of italy. And instead of imposing tributes to the conquered, they instead form a sort of alliance with them. These allies provided rome with recruits for their army. This made them very powerful and made them able to stand their ground in battle. After rome took most of italy, they had to face off a bigger and dangerous power than they have ever seen. Carthage, the rulers of the mediterranean. This started the Punic Wars (the romans called those who reside in carthage punics) they kept on fighting with the romans taking a lot of their land, until the romans finally went on and took Carthage. Officially taking the Punics. The romans also took Greece during this time and it had a very big impact to rome and it's culture. Greek became the second language of Rome, the infastructure of rome changed with the pillars, their government changed, etc. Other than the Greek city-states, Rome started giving citizenship to those who conquered.

Julius Caesar and Civil War (133 - 30 B.C)

In 49 B.C., Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus and Gaius Julius Caesar, 2 roman generals, had a huge civil war/conflict. After taking Gaul, the Germans, and some of Britain, Ceasar refused to submit to the senate. He then marched to Rome with his loyal Legions, and took it. He started a Civil War with Pompeius and ended up winning. During this, he annexed more land and made Cleopatra the Queen of Egypt. After some "fun" with Cleopatra, she went on to give birth to Ceasar's son. When he came back, he made himself Emperor of Rome and had control over the entire government. He was then assasinated (stabbed 23 times) by the senate. At his last breath, he gave all his money to his nephew, Gaius Octavius. He went on to fight those who were in the power struggle for the Roman Empire. After defeating his last rival, Mark Antony (Who probably had an affair with Cleopatra) who died by suicide, (with Cleopatra dying of suicide too) Octavius ruled the enire roman empire.

Augustus and More Expansion (27 B.C. - 96 AD)

Augustus (Octavius) ruled the Roman Empire for 46 years. He called himself a Princep Senatus (First Senator) as he didn't completetly abolish the republic, but instead took all possible positions. His rule was very peaceful and stability increased. The Roman Empire's economy went up during this time, construction boomed in rome with multiple buildings made out of concrete sprung up everywhere, and so many more. Yet, the people were still not happy with traffic jams and high rents. Temples were built worshippin Augustus, and even a month was named after him. With the later emperors (heriditary), Rome then continued expanding, with the control of more land in the mediterranean, more land in europe, and even part of Britain. Later on, the coliseum was opened by Titus making the people more and more happy.

The Good Emperors (96 - 180 AD)

The age of the 5 Good Emperors. The first is Nerva (96-98); he had many grand building projects like the temple of Venus and Roma, he made agrarian reforms, and he made a social system that supported poor children in rome. Trajan (98-117); one of the best emperors of rome (according to the senate). He expanded Rome's borders even more, he was generous to the populous and the senate, he increased the number of those who get free grain, he lessened taxes, rome became a million strong, many more. Hadrian (117-138); he was the defensive type. He built walls and fortfications in Britain and in the Rhines. Antoninus Pius (138-161); he was very, very peaceful. The days he reigned were known to be the most peaceful times of the Roman Empire. Marcus Aurlius (161-180); the last good emperor. He went on many military campaigns, but was more known now for his philosophical work/book Meditations.

The End of Ancient Rome (96 - 476 AD)

After the last good emperor Marcus Aurlius died, his succession was Commudus; his son. Commudus liked the gladiators glory rather than state affairs. He fought 735 times times in the arena. Commodus was then strangled by a champion gladiator, and since he didn't have any heirs, the empire descent into chaos. Patinax succeded Commmudus and was trying to be a good ruler. He was humble, experienced, and the people even loved him. He reversed a lot of what Commodus did and made it to be more in line with Marcus. The reason why he didn't rule for long was that he was changing things too quickly and he also angered the Praetorian Guard. The Praetorian Guard were the best of the best in rome and was tasked of defending and protecting the Emperor and Rome (was made by Augustus). Ironically, they are the reason why many emperors that succeded the age of the 5 failed. They were very corrupt and when Patinax was reversing what Commodus did, that included stripping some of the privileges the guard had. This obviously angered them so Patinax was assasinated. This continued where the Guard would auction the throne and kill the emperor if they had no need for them or they their changes did not align with them. In 212 AD, Emperor Caracalla made it so that all those who are free in the empire had roman citizenship. Later in 284 AD, Diocletian split the empire into 4 with 4 co-rulers with the 4 areas having their own capital. Rome lost it's signficance and the senate became a town council. After Diocletian resigned, the fight for power happened with Constantine the winner. He made the persicutions of Christians stop, and were allowed to build churches and temples. The capital of this new empire was Costantinople. Later in 15 years, Christianty became the official religion of the Roman Empire. The temples of Augustus and the Gods were destroyed, and the empire split. More infighting, control, and terror, and then eventually the empire came to the end in 476 AD with, ironically, the Emperor with the name of Romulus Augustulus.
